Getting brands to notice you

01

How brand deals actually work

Creators use "collab" loosely, but these are five distinct arrangements with different payoffs. Knowing which one you're being offered changes how you negotiate.

TypeWhat it meansDo you get paid
GiftingBrand sends free product, no obligation to postNo — the product is the payment
AffiliateYou share a link or code, earn a cut of resulting salesYes, only on sales you drive
Paid sponsorshipBrand pays a flat fee for agreed deliverablesYes, negotiated upfront
AmbassadorshipOngoing relationship, recurring contentYes, usually recurring
HybridFree product now, commission on future salesBoth

Most creators start at gifting and affiliate, then move toward paid sponsorship once their numbers and negotiating leverage grow.

02

Getting your first gifted products

Brands look for the same handful of signals before they send anything free — none of them require a huge following.

Consistency

A defined niche

Unboxing, GRWM, or lifestyle content, posted on a regular rhythm — not a one-off.

Engagement

A real audience

Comments and saves matter more than raw follower count at this stage.

Presentation

A clean bio

Business contact info visible — not buried in DMs.

Proof

A media kit

Even a one-pager signals you're treating this like a real channel.

How to get noticed

  • Tag brands genuinely in organic posts before ever pitching them — it puts you on their radar first.
  • Sign up directly for open affiliate or creator programs (Section 04).
  • Send a short, specific cold pitch (Section 05).
  • Join creator–brand marketplaces: ShopMy, LTK, Grin, Aspire, Creator.co.
03

Affiliate income — often the bigger earner

For unboxing and haul content specifically, affiliate commissions frequently outpace sponsorship fees, since you're already showing product on screen.

ProgramGood for
Amazon InfluencerBroad catalog, needs a qualifying following, gives you a storefront
LTKFashion, beauty, home — shoppable app-based links
ShopMyLower barrier to entry, growing brand list
Direct brand programsMany DTC brands run their own via ShareASale, Impact, or Refersion

How commissions work

  • Typically 3–20% of sale value, depending on category.
  • Paid on a delay — 30 to 60 days is standard, to account for returns.
  • Tracked through your unique link or code. Always use it — never post product without it.
04

Building a simple media kit

A media kit is the one-page document a brand uses to evaluate you before replying. Keep it to a single page.

What to include

  • Your name or brand name, with a professional photo
  • Follower counts by platform — brands can verify these, so keep it honest
  • Engagement rate, roughly: (likes + comments) ÷ followers
  • Audience demographics if available — age range, top locations
  • Two to four examples of past content
  • Your niche in one sentence, and a contact email

Canva has ready-made media kit templates if you want a fast starting point.

05

Pitching a brand cold

Brands are flooded with generic pitches. Specificity is what gets a reply.

Line 1

Who you are and your niche, in one sentence.

Line 2

Why this brand — mention an actual product of theirs.

Line 3

What you're proposing: an unboxing video, a GRWM feature, a specific format.

Line 4

A link to your media kit or recent content.

Line 5

A clear ask — gifted product, paid collaboration, or open to discuss.

Avoid vague asks like "would love to collab!" — a proposal a brand can say yes or no to quickly performs far better.

07

Money & legal basics

  • Disclosure is required, not optional. Gifted, paid, or affiliate content needs a clear #ad, #sponsored, or the platform's built-in paid-partnership tag — this is FTC law in the US, and most other countries have equivalents.
  • Track everything. Gifted product value, affiliate income, sponsorship fees. Gifted product can count as taxable income above a threshold depending on your country — worth a conversation with a tax preparer once this becomes regular.
  • Get agreements in writing. Even a short email confirming deliverables, payment, and usage rights protects both sides.
  • Negotiate usage rights separately. If a brand wants to reuse your content in their own ads, that's a bigger ask than a single post — and should come with more pay.

The unglamorous part

Legal & taxes

What Philippine content creators are actually required to do once brand deals and gifted product start coming in — registration, tax options, disclosure rules, and contracts.

This is not legal or tax advice. It's a starting map, built from the Bureau of Internal Revenue's Revenue Memorandum Circular No. 97-2021 (the circular that specifically addresses social media influencers), the Consumer Act of the Philippines (RA 7394), the Data Privacy Act (RA 10173), and current BIR registration guidance. Rules, thresholds, and forms change — verify anything before you file, and get a Philippine CPA or lawyer for your actual situation, especially once income becomes regular.
01

Yes, you need to register — regardless of income

The BIR's position, stated directly in RMC 97-2021, is that anyone earning income "in cash or in kind" from content on YouTube, Instagram, TikTok, Facebook, or similar platforms is classified as a self-employed individual running a trade or business. That includes sponsored posts, ad revenue, affiliate commissions, brand ambassador fees, and gifted products — gifted products are counted at fair market value even if no cash changes hands.

There's a common myth that income under ₱250,000 a year means you don't need to register. That threshold is where income tax starts being owed — it isn't a registration exemption. You're expected to register and file, even if the return shows zero tax due.

02

The registration checklist

Roughly in order. Most of this can now be done through BIR's Online Registration and Update System (ORUS) rather than an in-person RDO visit.

Step 1

Get a TIN if you don't already have one, via BIR Form 1901 or ORUS.

Step 2

Register a business name with the DTI — optional if you're operating under your own legal name, required if you want to use a brand/channel name on invoices.

Step 3

Get Barangay Clearance and a Mayor's Permit from your local government unit for your registered address (often your home).

Step 4

Complete BIR registration and receive your Certificate of Registration (COR / BIR Form 2303), which lists exactly which returns you're required to file.

Step 5

Register your books of accounts (BIR Form 1900) within 30 days of getting your COR — a simple manual ledger is enough to start.

Step 6

Set up invoicing — either BIR-printed invoices or your own via Authority to Print (ATP). Under current rules, invoices (not the old "Official Receipts") are the primary document the BIR recognizes.

Step 7

Register for SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG as a self-employed/voluntary member — as a self-employed creator you pay both the employee and employer share.

The ₱500 annual BIR registration fee was abolished starting 2024 under the Ease of Paying Taxes Act (RA 11976). You now only pay a ₱30 documentary stamp tax on initial registration.

03

Choosing how you're taxed

If your gross annual receipts don't exceed ₱3,000,000 (the VAT threshold), you choose between two options when you file — and once you pick the 8% option for the year, it's locked in until the next tax year.

OptionHow it worksBest for
8% flat rate8% of gross receipts above ₱250,000, with no separate Percentage Tax and no itemized deductionsLower expenses, simpler bookkeeping, most beginner creators
Graduated rates + Percentage Tax0–35% progressive income tax on net income after deducting actual business expenses, plus 3% quarterly Percentage Tax on grossHigh legitimate expenses (equipment, editors, travel) that would meaningfully lower taxable income

Worked example at the 8% rate: ₱600,000 in gross receipts for the year → 8% × (₱600,000 − ₱250,000) = ₱28,000 annual income tax. Once gross receipts cross ₱3,000,000 in any 12-month period, the graduated rates and 12% VAT registration become mandatory — this isn't optional past that point.

04

What counts as taxable income

Direct

Brand deals & ambassadorships

Flat fees, retainers, and ambassador payments.

Platform

Ad revenue

YouTube Partner Program and similar platform monetization.

Commission

Affiliate income

LTK, ShopMy, Amazon, TikTok Shop commissions.

In-kind

Gifted products

Counted at fair market value even with no cash payment.

Also counted: co-created product lines, digital products (courses, e-books, presets), and income from selling your own photos or videos. If a portion of your income comes from overseas platforms (YouTube AdSense paid from the US, for example), it's still generally taxable in the Philippines if you're a resident — and the BIR has said it can request income data from foreign tax authorities under information-exchange treaty provisions, so "they can't see my overseas earnings" isn't a safe assumption.

05

Deductible expenses (graduated-rate option only)

If you choose graduated rates instead of the 8% flat option, the BIR's own guidance names these as legitimate deductible business expenses for content creators — keep receipts for anything you'd claim.

  • Filming and production equipment (camera, lighting, mic, tripod)
  • Computer equipment and software or subscription licensing fees (editing apps, Canva Pro, cloud storage)
  • Internet and communication expenses
  • Home office costs — a reasonable portion of rent and utilities
  • Office and content supplies
  • Travel and transportation tied to content or brand work
  • Video editing, costume/wardrobe, and set design costs
  • Advertising and marketing spend
  • Depreciation on equipment
  • Bank charges and shipping fees

Under the 8% flat option you can't itemize these — the trade-off is simplicity for giving up deductions. Run the math both ways once your expenses are real, not hypothetical.

08

Disclosure & advertising rules

There's no single "influencer law" in the Philippines, but sponsored content is still covered by existing consumer protection and advertising rules — mainly the Consumer Act (RA 7394) and the Ad Standards Council's Code of Ethics, which the ASC administers as a self-regulatory body.

  • Disclosure is required whenever there's a "material connection" — payment, free product, commission, or any other perk tied to the post. This has to be clear and placed where a viewer will actually see it, not buried in hashtags at the end of a long caption.
  • For photo/caption content, the disclosure should sit in the image or the first part of the caption, not after several paragraphs.
  • For video, disclose in the title or near the start, and ideally repeat it if the video gets clipped or reposted elsewhere.
  • Claims must be substantiated. Before/after results, "no side effects," or performance claims need to be something you can actually back up — this applies to you as the endorser, not only to the brand.
  • Health, supplement, and food claims fall under additional FDA Philippines advertising rules on top of the general ASC code — treat brand-provided scripts in these categories with extra caution.
09

Other legal essentials

Data

Data Privacy Act (RA 10173)

If you run giveaways or collect follower info (emails, addresses for shipping), you're processing personal data and need consent and a basic privacy practice — not just a brand's problem.

IP

Copyright & music

Unlicensed music, images, or clips in sponsored content create real risk. The ASC code also requires advertisers and endorsers to respect others' intellectual property.

Contracts

What a brand contract should cover

Deliverables and deadlines, usage rights (can the brand "whitelist"/run ads with your content, and for how long), exclusivity terms, payment timeline, and withholding tax documentation via Form 2307.

Promotions

Giveaways & contests

"Like, follow, tag 3 friends" giveaways need clear mechanics — eligibility, duration, selection method, and prize delivery timeline — and can trigger extra scrutiny if structured like a raffle.

10

What non-compliance actually costs

  • Failure to register: fines generally ranging from ₱1,000 to ₱50,000
  • Late or unpaid tax: 25% surcharge plus 12% annual interest on the amount due
  • Tax evasion (deliberate non-declaration): the BIR has explicitly stated it can pursue civil penalties of 50% of the deficiency tax, and has run public campaigns naming influencer compliance as an enforcement priority since 2021

The BIR has said publicly it currently relies mostly on voluntary declaration for influencers rather than blanket audits — but it has also said it can request data from foreign platforms and tax treaty partners, and has issued formal investigation orders against specific creators before. "Small enough not to notice" isn't a compliance strategy.
